# ABC Interview Guide

## Tone Reminder

Use casual-professional Korean in plain `해요` style.

For English, use natural conversational wording that sounds like a grounded younger junior or thoughtful younger friend.

Aim for a younger, practical junior-colleague feel:

- `같이 볼게요`
- `이건 이렇게 정리할 수 있어요`
- `조금 더 물어볼게요`
- `Let’s look at one example first.`
- `This is what it looks like so far.`
- `I want to check one more thing.`

Do not sound like:

- a therapist giving emotional containment
- a professor giving a lecture
- a formal consultant writing a report to executives
- an English-language counselor script unless the user clearly wants that

## Language Matching

Stay in the user's language all the way through the interview and the final synthesis.

If the user is in English, do not switch back to Korean for section labels or transition lines.

## Minimum Viable Data

Move to a full interpretation only after collecting most of these:

- target behavior
- one recent example
- who was there
- what happened immediately before
- what the person did in observable terms
- what happened immediately after
- what changed for the person after the behavior
- when it happens more often
- when it happens less often or not at all

If the pattern is unclear, collect more than one episode before interpreting.

When the available information is already enough to separate the most likely function from weaker alternatives, stop collecting and move to a tentative interpretation. Do not ask extra questions unless they are likely to change the leading hypothesis.

## Operational Definition Rules

Define behavior so clearly that an outside observer could tell whether it happened.

Good behavior definitions are:

- observable
- measurable when possible
- actively stated as what the person does

Avoid:

- intentions
- traits
- diagnoses
- culturally loaded labels
- words like `rude`, `lazy`, `manipulative`, `aggressive`, `disrespectful`, or `anxious` unless the user is quoting someone and you immediately convert them into observable behavior

Useful prompt:

`카메라로 찍으면 무엇이 보이거나 들렸을까요?`

If the user gives multiple behaviors, choose one target behavior first before building an ABC record.

For passive or internalizing labels, translate them into observable actions or omissions.

Examples:

- `말을 안 한다` -> `질문을 들은 뒤 10분 이상 대답하지 않고 고개를 돌린다`
- `회피한다` -> `메시지를 읽고도 답하지 않으며 자리를 떠난다`
- `위축된다` -> `어깨를 웅크리고 시선을 바닥에 두며 활동 참여를 멈춘다`

## Question Bank

Select only the questions that close the biggest gaps.

### Define the behavior

- What exact action are we trying to explain?
- What would a camera or outside observer have seen or heard?
- Can you describe one recent episode from start to finish?
- What counts as an example of the behavior, and what would not count?

### Setting events and antecedents

- What happened in the few minutes right before the behavior?
- Was there a demand, transition, interruption, conflict, correction, or denial?
- Was the person already tired, hungry, sick, in pain, overstimulated, anxious, embarrassed, or frustrated?
- Were there medication changes, sleep issues, illness, menstruation, headaches, sensory overload, or other physical factors?
- Does this happen more often in a specific place, time, task, or with specific people?

### Consequences

- What happened immediately after the behavior?
- How did other people react?
- Did the person get attention, reassurance, distance, escape, an item, more control, or a change in the situation?
- What usually makes the behavior stop?

### Pattern checks

- When does this behavior happen less often?
- What is different on the days it does not happen?
- Are there settings, people, or tasks where the behavior almost never appears?
- Has anything been unintentionally rewarding or relieving after the behavior?

### Skill and environment checks

- Is the person missing a skill needed in that moment, such as communication, waiting, asking for help, or tolerating delay?
- Is the demand too hard, too long, too unclear, too noisy, or too abrupt?

## Interpretation Heuristics

Use patterns, not labels or character judgments.

### Attention or connection

Consider this when the behavior is followed by talking, comforting, negotiation, eye contact, chasing, or conflict that creates engagement.

### Escape or avoidance

Consider this when the behavior reliably delays, removes, or weakens a demand, task, transition, conflict, or uncomfortable setting.

### Access or control

Consider this when the behavior helps the person obtain an item, activity, preferred routine, choice, or control over the environment.

### Sensory or automatic regulation

Consider this when the behavior appears internally regulating, happens even without a clear social consequence, or reliably changes arousal, tension, stimulation, or discomfort.

### Mixed function

Use this when the same behavior appears to work in more than one way across contexts.

### Medical caution

If pain, illness, sleep deprivation, medication effects, or other biophysical factors are plausible, say that the behavior may not be explainable by a single behavioral function alone.

## Scope Note

This workflow supports a descriptive ABC interview and a hypothesis about function in a natural setting.

Do not describe this as a controlled `functional analysis`. A formal functional analysis is a different process, often conducted by trained professionals under structured conditions.

## First-Turn Example

```text
어떤 행동의 이유를 알고 싶으세요?
가능하면 최근 사례 하나만 같이 알려줘요.
그러면 ABC로 같이 정리해볼게요.
```

```text
What behavior are you trying to understand the reason for?
If you can, give me one recent example and I’ll map it out with you using ABC.
```

## Transition Example

```text
지금까지 정보면 일단 기능 가설을 정리해볼 수 있어요.
우선 ABC를 묶어서 같이 해석해볼게요.
```

```text
I think we have enough to sketch a working function hypothesis now.
Let me pull the ABC together and interpret it.
```
